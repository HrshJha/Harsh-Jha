# Design Brief

## 1. Design Vision

The portfolio should feel minimal, premium, technical, professional, calm, and intentional. It should communicate that Harsh Kumar Jha is building toward AI research engineering and production AI engineering through projects, engineering decisions, experience, and focused technical direction.

The design must not feel like a marketing landing page, visual playground, or generic student portfolio. It should feel like a quiet technical product surface: readable, structured, credible, and built with restraint.

Visitors should feel that the site respects their time. Recruiters should immediately understand where to go next. Hiring managers should see project work as the primary evidence layer. Research engineers should be able to identify relevance to AI safety, LLM evaluation, NLP systems, retrieval systems, and AI research engineering without parsing decorative content.

Visitors should remember:

- Harsh Kumar Jha.
- Building AI Products, Open Source & Real-World Solutions.
- Building autonomous AI systems through research, engineering, and product thinking.
- Projects are the primary proof of capability.
- The portfolio is evidence-led, not self-promotional.

## 2. Brand Personality

### Identity

The identity is that of an emerging AI/ML engineer with a long-term goal of becoming an AI Research Engineer and a secondary goal of becoming a Production AI Engineer.

The website must express:

- Engineering seriousness.
- Research orientation.
- Product thinking.
- Clear technical direction.
- Trust through restraint.

### Confidence Level

The confidence level should be quiet and evidence-based. The site should not overstate maturity, completion, impact, or status.

Allowed confidence:

- Clear project presentation.
- Clear technical focus areas.
- Clear experience highlights.
- Clear education and contact information.

Not allowed:

- Inflated claims.
- Unsupported superiority.
- Fake outcomes.
- Implied production usage for in-progress projects.
- Self-promotional language.

### Visual Personality

The visual personality should be:

- Minimal.
- Premium.
- Technical.
- Calm.
- Intentional.
- Compact but breathable.
- Clean and highly readable.

It should not be:

- Loud.
- Trend-driven.
- Decorative-first.
- Overdesigned.
- Generic.
- Visually noisy.

### Professional Personality

The professional personality should make recruiters and hiring managers feel that the site is organized, credible, and easy to evaluate. It should present Harsh as disciplined and focused without using unsupported claims.

### Engineering Personality

The engineering personality should prioritize systems over demos, architecture over screenshots, and execution over ideas. Visual emphasis should reinforce the decision hierarchy:

1. Projects.
2. Engineering decisions.
3. Experience.
4. About.
5. Skills.
6. Everything else.

### Research Personality

The research personality should feel methodical, precise, and grounded. The design should support careful reading and technical evaluation rather than spectacle.

## 3. Design Philosophy

### Minimalism

Minimalism means removing anything that does not increase credibility, clarity, trust, or technical depth. It does not mean empty pages or underdesigned sections. It means each visual element must have a job.

Approved minimalism:

- Clear hierarchy.
- Few competing focal points.
- Limited visual effects.
- Structured content.
- Enough whitespace to improve comprehension.

Rejected minimalism:

- Sparse content that hides project evidence.
- Excessively large empty areas.
- Decorative blankness.
- Reduced usability in the name of aesthetic restraint.

### Purpose

Every design choice must support one of the documented site outcomes:

- Increase AI/ML internship and research interview opportunities.
- Build credibility.
- Enable collaboration.

If a visual element does not support the recruiter journey, project evaluation, technical clarity, or contact path, it should be removed.

### Hierarchy

Hierarchy must follow the source documents. Projects are more important than biography. Experience is more important than skills. Skills are supporting evidence, not the main story.

The page should guide visitors through:

1. Who Harsh is.
2. What he is building.
3. What project evidence exists.
4. What relevant experience supports it.
5. What technical areas he works with.
6. How to review the resume or contact him.

### Precision

Precision should appear in alignment, spacing, typography, labels, and interaction states. Small inconsistencies will reduce trust because the product is positioned around engineering quality.

Precision requirements:

- Consistent section rhythm.
- Predictable navigation.
- Stable layout.
- Clear labels.
- Clean text alignment.
- No accidental overlaps.
- No ambiguous interactive elements.

### Balance

The design must balance technical density with scanability. It should not bury recruiters in detail, and it should not oversimplify the project layer for technical evaluators.

Balance rules:

- Hero should be concise.
- Projects should receive the strongest visual presence.
- Experience should be scannable.
- About should be compact and identity-focused.
- Skills should support scanning, not dominate.
- Resume and Contact should be obvious but not oversized.

### Whitespace

Whitespace should be compact but breathable. It should create separation and comprehension, not a decorative sense of emptiness.

Whitespace should:

- Improve section recognition.
- Reduce cognitive load.
- Give project cards or project blocks enough room to be evaluated.
- Preserve a calm reading pace.

Whitespace should not:

- Make the site feel sparse.
- Push critical proof too far below the fold.
- Create oversized hero areas that hide the next section.

Exact spacing scale: MISSING INFORMATION

### Consistency

Repeated content types must share consistent structure:

- Project summaries.
- Experience entries.
- Skill groups.
- Education facts.
- Navigation links.
- Contact links.
- Status labels.

Consistency is required because the portfolio must feel engineered rather than assembled from disconnected sections.

### Visual Rhythm

Visual rhythm should create a steady progression through the recruiter journey. Alternating density is acceptable only when it improves comprehension.

Expected rhythm:

- Clear opening statement.
- Strong project evidence.
- Compact professional proof.
- Focused identity and philosophy.
- Supporting skills and education.
- Clear resume and contact path.

### Information Density

Information density should be moderate. This is not a poster site and not a dense internal dashboard.

Use higher density for:

- Project summaries.
- Experience highlights.
- Skills.
- Education facts.

Use lower density for:

- Hero.
- Section transitions.
- Resume and contact CTAs.

### Design Trade-offs

When design goals conflict, use this order:

1. Clarity over novelty.
2. Credibility over visual drama.
3. Technical depth over decoration.
4. Scanability over exhaustive explanation.
5. Accessibility over subtle aesthetics.
6. Performance over heavy effects.

## 4. Emotional Goals

After using the website, visitors should feel:

- Trust: The site is factual, restrained, and source-backed.
- Respect: The work is presented with seriousness and care.
- Technical confidence: The project-first structure makes engineering capability easier to evaluate.
- Curiosity: The projects and focus areas invite deeper review without exaggeration.
- Professionalism: The site feels ready for recruiters, hiring managers, research engineers, founders, and collaborators.
- Calm: The interface is readable and not visually aggressive.
- Clarity: The next action is obvious at each major point.
- Focus: The portfolio has a clear AI/ML and research engineering direction.
- Discipline: The absence of vanity elements reinforces taste and judgment.
- Credibility: The site avoids unsupported achievements, fake metrics, and inflated language.

## 6. Visual Language

### Shape Language

The shape language should be simple, geometric, and restrained. Shapes should support content grouping, interaction, and hierarchy.

Allowed shape qualities:

- Clean rectangles.
- Subtle rounded corners.
- Straight alignment.
- Clear containers where grouping is needed.

Avoid:

- Organic blobs.
- Decorative shapes.
- Excessive rounded pills.
- Novel ornamental frames.

### Geometry

Geometry should feel precise and grid-based. Layout geometry should make the page feel engineered.

Rules:

- Align repeated elements to a shared grid.
- Use consistent widths for comparable content groups.
- Avoid arbitrary offsets unless they clarify hierarchy.
- Avoid asymmetry that reads as accidental.

### Depth

Depth should be shallow. The site should not rely on heavy shadows, glass layers, or floating effects.

Depth may be used for:

- Interactive states.
- Modest separation between project cards and background.
- Dialogs or overlays if required by future implementation.

Depth must not become the primary visual style.

### Layering

Layering should be functional. Content layers should clarify hierarchy, not create visual spectacle.

Allowed:

- Navigation above content.
- Focus states above normal states.
- Modest card or container separation.

Not allowed:

- Stacked translucent layers as a dominant effect.
- Decorative floating panels.
- Unnecessary background layers.

### Cards

Cards are appropriate for repeated individual items such as projects and experience entries. Cards should not become decorative wrappers around every section.

Card rules:

- Use cards where comparison or repetition matters.
- Keep project cards visually stronger than skill cards.
- Do not nest cards inside cards.
- Do not make whole page sections look like floating cards.
- Avoid oversized decorative cards that weaken information density.

### Containers

Containers should group related content only when grouping improves comprehension.

Use containers for:

- Project summaries.
- Experience entries.
- Skill groups.
- Resume/contact blocks if needed.

Do not use containers for:

- Every paragraph.
- Decorative page bands without purpose.
- Visual filler.

### Borders

Borders should be subtle and structural. They can define cards, separate sections, and support scanning.

Border rules:

- Use consistently.
- Keep visual weight restrained.
- Do not combine heavy borders with heavy shadows unless functionally necessary.

### Corners

Corners should feel modern but controlled. Extreme corner rounding is not aligned with the technical, professional direction.

Spacing must create a compact but breathable reading flow.

Spacing rules:

- Hero should not consume excessive vertical space.
- The next section should be discoverable without forcing unnecessary scrolling.
- Projects should receive more space than supporting sections.
- Skills and education should be compact.

## 9. Motion Philosophy

Motion must be purposeful, smooth, narrative-driven, and never decorative. Every animation must clarify hierarchy, state, transition, or user action.

### Purpose

Allowed motion purposes:

- Confirm interaction.
- Show navigation context.
- Clarify section entry.
- Support content sequencing.
- Preserve continuity during page changes.
- Improve perceived responsiveness.

Not allowed:

- Motion that exists only to impress.
- Constant background movement.
- Decorative animated patterns.
- Typing effects.
- Particle systems.
- Motion that delays access to content.

### Timing

Motion should be fast enough to respect recruiter scanning and smooth enough to feel premium.

Exact timing values: MISSING INFORMATION

### Speed

Animation speed should feel controlled and calm. Avoid abrupt jumps and slow theatrical transitions.

### Rhythm

Motion rhythm should match the page hierarchy. Important content may enter with more deliberate sequencing, but supporting content should not compete for attention.

### Physics

Physics should feel subtle and precise. Avoid bouncy, playful, exaggerated, or elastic motion unless future source direction changes.

### Transitions

Transitions should help visitors understand where they are and what changed.

Allowed:

- Subtle opacity changes.
- Subtle position changes.
- Clear focus transitions.
- Page transition continuity if performance remains strong.

Avoid:

- Large parallax effects.
- Rotations.
- Dramatic zooms.
- Effects that make text harder to read.

### Hover

Hover states should confirm interactivity without changing layout.

Rules:

- Hover must not move content enough to cause layout shift.
- Hover must not hide text.
- Hover should not become the primary way to reveal essential information.

### Scrolling

Scrolling should feel natural. Scroll effects must not block content access or create a storytelling gimmick.

Allowed:

- Subtle section reveal if it improves orientation.
- Sticky navigation if implemented accessibly.

Not allowed:

- Scroll hijacking.
- Scroll-dependent content that hides required information.
- Excessive parallax.

### Navigation

Navigation motion should be direct and functional. Mobile navigation should open and close clearly, with focus management.
